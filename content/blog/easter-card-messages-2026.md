  <section id="the-problem">
    <h2>Why Most Easter Card Messages Fall Flat</h2>
    <p>Generic Easter messages—"Happy Easter!" scrawled inside a store-bought card—are forgotten the same day they're opened. The problem is a mismatch between the message, the recipient, and the occasion. A deeply religious blessing sent to a secular colleague feels awkward. A silly egg pun sent to a grieving family member misreads the room entirely.</p>
    <p>The other common failure is copying messages that are too long, too vague, or too obviously pulled from a template. Recipients can tell when the words weren't chosen for them specifically. What works is a short, intentional message that demonstrates you considered who you were writing to. That's exactly what the framework below delivers.</p>
  </section>

  <section id="step-by-step">
    <h2>Step-by-Step: How to Choose the Right Easter Message</h2>

    <h3>Step 1 — Identify Your Recipient's Category</h3>
    <p>Before writing anything, place the recipient in one of five categories: religious family/friends, secular family/friends, children, professional contacts, or distant acquaintances. This single decision eliminates 80% of your options immediately and points you toward the right section below.</p>

    <h3>Step 2 — Determine the Tone of Your Card Design</h3>
    <p>Your message must match the visual tone of the card. A card with pastel watercolor florals calls for a soft, heartfelt message. A card with a cartoon bunny calls for wordplay. A card with a cross or sunrise calls for reverent, faith-centered language. Mismatched tone-design combinations undermine both elements.</p>

    <h3>Step 3 — Choose Message Length Based on Relationship Depth</h3>
    <p>Closer relationships warrant longer, more personal messages. Colleagues and acquaintances get short, warm greetings—one to two sentences. Immediate family and close friends can receive two to four sentences. Long messages work only when every sentence adds genuine meaning; padding degrades sincerity.</p>

    <h3>Step 4 — Personalize at Least One Detail</h3>
    <p>Add one specific detail that makes the message unmistakably for this person: a shared memory, a reference to something they're going through, or an acknowledgment of your specific relationship. Even a single personalized line transforms a template message into something memorable.</p>

    <h3>Step 5 — Match Your Greeting to the Card's Visual Design</h3>
    <p>Review the complete message one final time against the card design. Check: Does the font size leave room for the message? Is the text color readable against the background? Does the message reinforce—not contradict—the visual mood? Adjust as needed before printing or sending.</p>

    <div class="practical-example">
      <h3>Practical Example</h3>
      <p><strong>Recipient:</strong> Your grandmother who is devout, lives far away, and recently lost her husband.</p>
      <p><strong>Chosen message:</strong> "May the hope of Easter morning bring you comfort and remind you that love never truly ends. Thinking of you with so much love this Easter."</p>
      <p><strong>Why it works:</strong> It acknowledges her faith, her grief, and your relationship—without being heavy or generic. It took 10 seconds of personalization to make a template message feel personal.</p>

    <h2>Professional Tips for Better Easter Cards in 2026</h2>

    <p>These are the practices that separate forgettable cards from ones that get saved or displayed.</p>

    <p><strong>Write the message before choosing the design.</strong> Most people do it backwards. When you start with the words, the design choice becomes obvious and the result is more cohesive.</p>

    <p><strong>Use AI image generation for original visuals.</strong> Stock Easter imagery is everywhere. An AI-generated image created to match your specific message is distinctive and costs nothing extra in 2026.</p>

    <p><strong>Send digital cards earlier than printed ones.</strong> Digital Easter cards sent 3–5 days before Easter arrive at the right moment. Printed cards should be mailed 7–10 days in advance to account for delivery.</p>

    <p><strong>Avoid the word "just" in professional messages.</strong> "Just wanted to wish you a Happy Easter" diminishes the gesture. State it directly: "Wishing you a Happy Easter."</p>

    <p><strong>Sign with relationship context for large card lists.</strong> If you're sending to a large list and recipients may not immediately recognize you, add a brief identifier: "— Sarah (from the marketing team)" or "— The Johnsons (next door)."</p>

  <!-- COMMON MISTAKES -->
  <section id="common-mistakes">
    <h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

    <p><strong>Sending religious messages to secular recipients without knowing their orientation.</strong> A Christ-centered message sent to someone who doesn't observe Easter religiously can create awkward distance rather than warmth. When in doubt, use secular renewal-focused language.</p>

    <p><strong>Over-personalizing professional messages.</strong> Referencing shared personal details in a business Easter card crosses a line unless you have a genuinely personal relationship outside work. Keep professional messages warm but generically applicable.</p>

    <p><strong>Using emoji-heavy messages in printed cards.</strong> Emojis work on screens. In printed form they look dated and unprofessional. For digital cards, use sparingly and only in social or casual contexts.</p>

    <p><strong>Copying long template messages verbatim without any personal modification.</strong> Even one added sentence—"This Easter especially, thinking of your family after the move"—transforms a template into a genuine message.</p>

    <p><strong>Ignoring font readability over patterned backgrounds.</strong> A beautiful card that can't be read is a failed card. Always test your text against the full card design at print or screen size before finalizing.</p>
  </section>
