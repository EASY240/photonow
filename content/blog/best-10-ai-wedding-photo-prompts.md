  <section id="the-problem">
    <h2>The Problem with Generic Wedding Prompts</h2>
    <p>
      Generic text prompts generate imagery with artificial aesthetics — "waxy" skin textures that look like porcelain rather than human skin, fabric that drapes with impossible physics, and lighting that has no identifiable source or direction. The result is immediately recognizable as AI-generated: technically composed but visually unconvincing.
    </p>
    <p>
      Couples and creators require professional-looking editorial portraits without the cost of traditional photography packages — which regularly reach $3,000–$8,000 for a full wedding day. Bridging this gap requires technical prompt structures that emulate high-end camera hardware and film stocks. The AI model is not a photographer making lighting decisions; it is a pattern-matching system. Give it the technical parameters of a real photograph and it produces output that resembles one. Give it vague emotional descriptors and it defaults to its highest-frequency training pattern — which is generic, over-processed, and synthetic.
    </p>
    <p>
      The same principle applies when editing real wedding photos. Using the <a href="https://modernphototools.com/tools/ai-portrait" target="_blank">AI Portrait tool</a> for skin enhancement or the <a href="https://modernphototools.com/tools/remove-background" target="_blank">Background Remover</a> for environment swaps requires the same level of specificity in the edit instruction. For a full framework on structured prompt engineering across all photo types, see the <a href="https://modernphototools.com/blog/master-ai-photo-editing-prompts" target="_blank">master AI photo editing prompts guide</a>.
    </p>

  <section id="prompt-anatomy">
    <h2>Anatomy of a Professional Wedding Prompt</h2>
    <p>Every effective wedding AI prompt contains six layers. Each layer removed reduces output quality predictably.</p>

    <div style="margin:24px 0;">
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">1</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Scene &amp; Subject</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Define the environment and subject with visual specificity. Not "wedding photo" — "bride standing in the center aisle of a grand cathedral" or "couple on a vintage wooden boat on Lake Como."</p></div>
      </div>
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">2</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Material &amp; Garment Physics</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Describe fabric weight, construction, and surface. "Cathedral-length veil in an ethereal cloud of lace and tulle" tells the model exactly how the fabric moves and reflects light. "White dress" does not.</p></div>
      </div>
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">3</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Lighting Pattern</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Name the lighting technique: Rembrandt, golden hour, alpenglow, direct flash, stained glass diffusion. Each produces a specific shadow pattern and color temperature the model replicates accurately.</p></div>
      </div>
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">4</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Optical Specification</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Specify the camera body, focal length, aperture, and ISO. "Canon EOS R5, 85mm f/1.2 lens, ISO 200, f/1.8" gives the model the exact depth-of-field compression and bokeh quality that lens produces.</p></div>
      </div>
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">5</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Skin &amp; Texture Realism</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Add skin texture descriptors to prevent the uncanny valley effect: "visible pores," "subtle peach fuzz," "natural skin oiliness," "Kodak Portra 400 aesthetic." These override the model's default beauty-filter behavior.</p></div>
      </div>
      <div style="display:flex;gap:16px;align-items:flex-start;margin-bottom:16px;padding:18px;background:#f8f9fa;border-radius:8px;border-left:4px solid #6366f1;">
        <div style="min-width:34px;height:34px;background:#6366f1;color:#fff;border-radius:50%;display:flex;align-items:center;justify-content:center;font-weight:700;flex-shrink:0;">6</div>
        <div><h3 style="margin:0 0 4px;color:#1f2937;">Negative Prompt Exclusions</h3><p style="margin:0;color:#555;font-size:0.97rem;">Explicitly exclude: "plastic skin, waxy texture, doll-like symmetry, over-smoothed, airbrushed, artificial lighting, HDR look." In Midjourney use <code>--no</code>, in Stable Diffusion use the negative prompt field.</p></div>
      </div>
    </div>

  <section id="pro-tips">
    <h2>Professional Tips</h2>

    <h3>Specify Optics by Focal Length and Purpose</h3>
    <p>
      Focal length is not decorative — it determines how the model renders spatial compression and background blur. Use <strong>85mm f/1.2</strong> for bridal portraits: it produces flattering facial compression and a creamy, separated background. Use <strong>35mm</strong> for dance floor and reception candids: it captures environmental context and natural distortion at close range. Use <strong>wide-angle 35mm</strong> for landscape elopements where the environment is the primary subject. Use <strong>50mm f/1.4</strong> for natural-perspective ceremony scenes. See <a href="https://www.bhphotovideo.com/explora/photography/tips-and-solutions/focal-length-guide" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">B&H's focal length guide</a> for reference on how each lens renders real scenes.
    </p>

    <h3>Define Material Physics Precisely</h3>
    <p>
      Fabric behavior is one of the most common AI failure points. Replace generic terms with construction-specific descriptions: <strong>"voluminous tulle overskirt"</strong> (light, airy, layered), <strong>"beaded lace bodice"</strong> (heavy, structured, light-catching), <strong>"structured crepe gown"</strong> (matte, architectural, minimal drape), <strong>"silk charmeuse with bias cut"</strong> (fluid, clinging, high-sheen). Each term activates a different set of material-physics patterns in the model's training data. The same principle applies to men's attire: <strong>"ivory raw silk sherwani with minimal embroidery"</strong> produces a completely different output than <strong>"traditional Indian groom."</strong>
    </p>

    <h3>Control Lighting Patterns by Name</h3>
    <p>
      Named lighting patterns produce predictable, technically accurate output. <strong>Rembrandt lighting</strong>: one main light source at 45° above and to the side, creating a triangular highlight on the shadowed cheek — use for studio dramatic portraits. <strong>Golden hour</strong>: warm 3200K directional side light just above horizon level — use for all outdoor romantic scenes. <strong>Direct flash</strong>: harsh, flat frontal illumination with sharp shadows behind the subject — use for candid reception/dance floor shots. <strong>Alpenglow</strong>: pink-violet diffused light from reflected mountain snow — use for alpine elopements. Name the pattern, and the model handles shadow direction, color temperature, and fill automatically.
    </p>

    <h3>Engineer Skin Texture to Avoid the Uncanny Valley</h3>
    <p>
      AI models default to over-smoothed, beauty-filtered skin unless explicitly instructed otherwise. Add these terms to any portrait prompt: <strong>"visible pores," "subtle peach fuzz," "natural skin oiliness," "authentic skin texture."</strong> For a film-analog look, add: <strong>"Kodak Portra 400 aesthetic," "fine film grain," "peachy warm skin tones."</strong> Combine these with negative prompts: in Midjourney, append <code>--no plastic skin, waxy texture, airbrushed, doll-like symmetry, over-smoothed</code>. The skin texture modifiers are especially important for close-up and medium-close shots where skin quality dominates the visual quality of the image.
    </p>

    <h3>Use Negative Prompts as a Second Control Layer</h3>
    <p>
      Negative prompts remove the model's default failure modes rather than adding new instructions. For wedding photography, always exclude: <code>plastic skin, waxy texture, artificial lighting, HDR look, over-saturated, doll-like symmetry, digital painting, cartoon, illustration, 3D render</code>. In Midjourney, append <code>--no [terms]</code> after the main prompt. In Stable Diffusion, paste these into the dedicated negative prompt field. In DALL-E 3, include "avoid" phrasing within the main prompt text. Negative prompting is not optional for professional-quality outputs — it is the mechanism that prevents the model from reverting to its default aesthetic.
    </p>
  </section>
